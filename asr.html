Age Standardised Rates

Data Sources

All data was sourced from the Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS) Census of Population of Housing Application Programming Interface (API). Specifically, long term health conditions were available only for 2021, while core activity need for assistance was available for the full data series.

2021 Australian Statistical Geography Standard (ASGS) areas were used for all boundaries, visualised at the Statisical Area 2 (SA2) level. That is, while historical data is visualised for 2011 and 2016, these have been populated according to the updated 2021 ASGS boundaries in the time series tables.

Included variables

The variables chosen for estimation were informed from the Census. In the case of core activity assistance, this was chosen due to being one of the only questions on long term health conditions that has been asked consistently and is thus available as a time series. All other long term health conditions were included in the 2021 Census for the first time, and all variables were thus included.

Method

Variables were age standardised using the June 2001 standard Australian population, as recommended by the ABS and the Australian Institute of Health and Welfare (AIHW). The Census API categorises age slightly differently for long term health conditions and core activity need for assistance, with long term health conditions categorising age into nine groups (0-14, 15-24, and then ten year age groups up to 85+), while core activity need for assistance categorises age into eleven groups (0-4, 5-14, 15-19, 20-24, and then ten year age groups up to 85+). Age groupings were used as supplied by the API, with the standard population aggregated accordingly.

Usual resident populations were used to standardise the estimates in order to match the numerator. That is, census counts were used without additional correction for undercounting (e.g. Estimated Resident Populations), as there are no equivalent corrected estimates for the conditions enumerated.

Direct age standardisation was used, with confidence intervals calculated using the epitools package in R, which employs a method based on the gamma distribution as described by Fay and Feuer (1997).

Note that only SA2s for with sufficient population for valid estimation were included. This meant excluding the bottom 5% of SA2s by total population for each year, which included mostly non-residential SA2s, such as ‘Sydney Airport’ or ‘Rookwood Cemetery’.

Limitations

All data reported in the census are self-reported, with no validation or cross reference to registry and/or administrative data. The validity of self-reported estimates of long-term health conditions has been shown to be questionable, and variable according to condition (e.g. Smith et al, 2008), and this should be taken into account when interpreting these estimates. Further validation is required before these estimates can be taken at face value.

In addition, inconsistent age grouping may lead to some bias in the estimates, for example if a condition is especially prevalent in an age group that has been aggregated. The AIHW recommend generally using five-year age groups, however these were unavailable in the current dataset.
