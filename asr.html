Introduction

The Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS) Census of Population and Housing (the Census) provides summaries by area across many key demographic variables. However, areas enumerated in the Census are defined by the ABS and

  • Are adjusted from year to year, precluding simple longitudinal assessment; and
  • Are not defined at non-ABS area boundaries, such as Local Health District

With the 2021 Census, the ABS has released specific time series Census datasets to allow for longitudinal comparisons in Census variables across consistent geographic boundaries. This allows for direct and valid comparisons to be made across Census variables for a given area over time, without need for conversion or aggregation.

The Public Health Research Analytics and Methods for Evidence unit (PHRAME) has launched an online platform for the visualisation of this time series Census data from 2011, 2016 and 2021, covering three broad topics -

  • Demographic Variables, such as population size, income and age
  • Broad indicators of Cultural and Linguistic Diversity, such as language spoken at home and country of birth
  • ABS indexes of socioeconomic status
  • Self-reported long-term health conditions, included in 2021 for the first time

Specific variables were selected using a combination of relevance to clinical and non-clinical services within SLHD and those that have been reported in previous Census summaries, among those that were available across time. All variables with the exception of the newly included self-reported long-term health conditions are reported across the three most recent censuses (specifically 2011, 2016 and 2021).

Method - Age Standardised Rates

Data Sources

All data was sourced from the Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS) Census of Population of Housing Application Programming Interface (API). Specifically, long term health conditions were available only for 2021, while core activity need for assistance was available for the full data series.

Statistical method

Variables were age standardised using the June 2001 standard Australian population, as recommended by the ABS and the Australian Institute of Health and Welfare (AIHW). The Census API categorises age slightly differently for long term health conditions and core activity need for assistance, with long term health conditions categorising age into nine groups (0-14, 15-24, and then ten year age groups up to 85+), while core activity need for assistance categorises age into eleven groups (0-4, 5-14, 15-19, 20-24, and then ten year age groups up to 85+). Age groupings were used as supplied by the API, with the standard population aggregated accordingly.

Direct age standardisation was used, with confidence intervals calculated using the epitools package in R, which employs a method based on the gamma distribution as described by Fay and Feuer (1997).

Only SA2s for with sufficient population for valid estimation were included. This meant excluding the bottom 5% of SA2s by total population for each year, which included mostly non-residential SA2s, such as ‘Sydney Airport’ or ‘Rookwood Cemetery’.

Limitations

All data reported in the census are self-reported, with no validation or cross reference to registry and/or administrative data. The validity of self-reported estimates of long-term health conditions has been shown to be questionable, and variable according to condition (e.g. Smith et al, 2008), and this should be taken into account when interpreting these estimates. Further validation is required before these estimates can be taken at face value.

Usual resident populations were used to standardise the estimates in order to match the numerator. That is, census counts were used without additional correction for undercounting (e.g. Estimated Resident Populations), as there are no equivalent corrected estimates for the conditions enumerated. This is unlikely to introduce substantial bias to the estimates, unless particular conditions are overrepresented in certain populations known be undercounted.

In addition, inconsistent age grouping may lead to some bias in the estimates, for example if a condition is especially prevalent in an age group that has been aggregated. The AIHW recommend generally using five-year age groups, however these were unavailable in the current dataset.
