Introduction

The Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS) Census of Population and Housing (the Census) provides summaries by area across many key demographic variables. However, areas enumerated in the Census are defined by the ABS and

  • Are adjusted from year to year, precluding simple longitudinal assessment; and
  • Are not defined at non-ABS area boundaries, such as Local Health District

With the 2021 Census, the ABS has released specific time series Census datasets to allow for longitudinal comparisons in Census variables across consistent geographic boundaries. This allows for direct and valid comparisons to be made across Census variables for a given area over time, without need for conversion or aggregation.

The Public Health Research Analytics and Methods for Evidence unit (PHRAME) has launched an online platform for the visualisation of this time series Census data from 2011, 2016 and 2021, covering three broad topics -

  • Demographic Variables, such as population size, income and age
  • Broad indicators of Cultural and Linguistic Diversity, such as language spoken at home and country of birth
  • ABS indexes of socioeconomic status
  • Self-reported long-term health conditions, included in 2021 for the first time

Specific variables were selected using a combination of relevance to clinical and non-clinical services within SLHD and those that have been reported in previous Census summaries, among those that were available across time. All variables with the exception of the newly included self-reported long-term health conditions are reported across the three most recent censuses (specifically 2011, 2016 and 2021).

Method - Cultural and Linguistic Diversity

Data Sources

All data was sourced from the Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS) Census of Population of Housing Application Programming Interface (API). Specifically, both country of birth of person (BPLP) and language used at home (LANP) were used for the full data series.

The top 10 countries of birth (excluding Australia), and top 10 languages other than English spoken at home, were calculated by population within the Sydney Local Health District. These specific countries and languages are visualised separately at the SA2 level.

Statistical method

For proportions, numerators were calculated as the total population within that subcategory, while the denominator was the total population with a valid response. In this way, all estimates have a meaningful complement. For example, if 42% of a population in an SA2 are born overseas, it can be assumed that 58% of that area were born in Australia. That is, the proportions will sum to 100%. Similarly, if 12% of the population in an area were to speak Spanish as a preferred language at home, it can be assumed that 88% of that population do not speak Spanish at home.

Only SA2s for with sufficient population for valid estimation were included. This meant excluding the bottom 5% of SA2s by total population for each year, which included mostly non-residential SA2s, such as ‘Sydney Airport’ or ‘Rookwood Cemetery’.

Limitations

All data reported in the census are self-reported, with no validation or cross reference to registry and/or administrative data. The validity of self-reported estimates of Cultural and Linguistic Diversity may be subject to bias, if certain populations are less likely to answer these questions. As only valid responses were used in the denominator, results would be biased if certain population were less likely to be included in the total (i.e. total valid responses). This should be considered when interpreting the results.

More broadly, Census counts are known to undercount certain populations (e.g. Estimated Resident Populations), and there are no suitably corrected estimates available for the included CALD variables. This is unlikely to introduce substantial bias to the estimates, unless counting particular populations known be undercounted.

Finally, CALD is a complex and multifaceted concept, capturing diversity across myriad aspects, including culture, community, religion and many more. The variables available in the Census give a very broad overview of this diversity, with full acknowledgement that many important aspects are not covered.
