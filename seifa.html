Introduction

The Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS) Census of Population and Housing (the Census) provides summaries by area across many key demographic variables. However, areas enumerated in the Census are defined by the ABS and

  • Are adjusted from year to year, precluding simple longitudinal assessment; and
  • Are not defined at non-ABS area boundaries, such as Local Health District

With the 2021 Census, the ABS has released specific time series Census datasets to allow for longitudinal comparisons in Census variables across consistent geographic boundaries. This allows for direct and valid comparisons to be made across Census variables for a given area over time, without need for conversion or aggregation.

The Public Health Research Analytics and Methods for Evidence unit (PHRAME) has launched an online platform for the visualisation of this time series Census data from 2011, 2016 and 2021, covering three broad topics -

  • Demographic Variables, such as population size, income and age
  • Broad indicators of Cultural and Linguistic Diversity, such as language spoken at home and country of birth
  • ABS indexes of socioeconomic status
  • Self-reported long-term health conditions, included in 2021 for the first time

Specific variables were selected using a combination of relevance to clinical and non-clinical services within SLHD and those that have been reported in previous Census summaries, among those that were available across time. All variables with the exception of the newly included self-reported long-term health conditions are reported across the three most recent censuses (specifically 2011, 2016 and 2021).

Method - Socioeconomic Index For Areas (SEIFA)

Data Sources

All data was sourced from the Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS) Census of Population of Housing Application Programming Interface (API). Specifically, all SEIFA indexes enumerated by the ABS were included. Broadly, the four indexes are:

  • the Index of Relative Socio-economic Advantage and Disadvantage (IRSAD), focussing on both advantage and disadvantage
  • the Index of Relative Socio-economic Disadvantage (IRSD), focussing on relative socio-economic disadvantage
  • the Index of Education and Occupation (IEO), focussing on relative Education and Occupation advantage and disadvantage, including variables detailing educational attainment, occupation, income, and employment
  • the Index of Economic Resources (IER), focussing on Economic advantage and disadvantage (e.g. housing as well as income).

Statistical method

Raw SEIFA variable scores were sourced at the SA1 level for their respective Census (e.g. 2011 SEIFA scores for 2011 SA1 boundaries), along with Usual Resident Populations (URPs) for these areas. SA1 boundaries for 2011 and 2016 were intersected with 2021 SA2 boundaries, with a threshold of 50% taken for inclusion. That is, if an SA1 was at least 50% within the corresponding 2021 SA2, it was assigned to that area. In almost all cases this resulted in a one-to-one match. Scores were then population weighted using URPs to obtain an overall score for the 2021 SA2, with population weighted scores ranked and categorised into deciles.

This method was adapted from the standard approach used by the ABS for assigning indexes within each census year.

Note that only SA2s for with sufficient population for valid estimation were included. This meant excluding the bottom 5% of SA2s by total population for each year, which included mostly non-residential SA2s, such as ‘Sydney Airport’ or ‘Rookwood Cemetery’.

Overall LHD estimates were calculated as the proportion of SA2s in an LHD in the highest two quintiles (i.e. the top 40% of all scores). Note that across the state there should be exactly 40% of all SA2s within these two quintiles (for scores indexed at the state level). Hence, any proportion higher or lower than 40% this for a specific LHD, can be directly compared to the distribution across the state for that year.

Limitations

SEIFA indexes are calculated differently each year, and are not intended to be used for time series analyses. Nonetheless, methods for most indexes have changed only slightly between 2016 and 2021, and so superficial comparisons may be made, albeit cautiously.

Socioeconomic status is a highly complex issue and no one index, nor combination of indexes, is intended to capture the full spectrum of socioeconomic advantage and disadvantage. Indexes are designed to be indicative only, with full acknowledgement that important factors influencing socioeconomic status may not be adequately captured by any of these indexes.
